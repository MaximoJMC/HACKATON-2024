Is climate change real?

Since the late 19th century, Earth's temperature has risen approximately 2 degrees Fahrenheit (1 degree Celsius), primarily due to human activities. According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), this influence has transitioned from a theory to a proven fact. Scientific evidence includes data from ice cores demonstrating an increase in carbon dioxide (CO₂) since the Industrial Revolution, a rate 250 times faster than during natural periods. The ocean has absorbed much of this additional heat, increasing its temperature and impacting ecosystems.


Ice sheets in Greenland and Antarctica are shrinking, contributing to rising sea levels, which have increased approximately 8 inches (20 centimeters) in the past century. Additionally, extreme weather events, such as heat waves and heavy rainfall, are becoming more frequent.


Climate change is a global crisis that demands immediate action. Transitioning to renewable energy sources and reducing emissions are essential to ensure a sustainable future. Despite the challenges, we have the capacity to alter our trajectory and protect our planet.

Atmospheric CO₂ Increase

Since the Industrial Revolution, the acidity of ocean surface waters has increased by approximately 30%. This increase is due to the oceans absorbing a significant portion of carbon dioxide emissions, which affects marine ecosystems.

20%

Sea Level Rise

Sea level has risen by about 20% in the last century. However, in the last two decades, the rate of increase has accelerated, doubling the rate of the past century, posing a growing risk to coastal communities and infrastructure.

1°C

Global Warming

The average temperature of the Earth's surface has increased by approximately 1°C (2 degrees Fahrenheit) since the late 19th century. This change is primarily driven by the increase in greenhouse gas emissions, and most of the warming has occurred in the last 40 years.

Nations on Alert: Global Temperature Rise

In 2024, the increase in global temperatures has been notably higher compared to previous years, and various reliable sources such as NASA and National Geographic have documented this phenomenon. Here’s a summary of the most relevant data: May 2024 was recorded as the hottest month in modern history, marking a full year of monthly temperature records. This indicates an alarming trend in global warming. According to NASA, the average surface temperature of the planet has increased by approximately 2 degrees Fahrenheit (1 degree Celsius) since the late 19th century. This increase is primarily attributed to human activities, such as the burning of fossil fuels. The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) reported that 2023 was the warmest year ever recorded, and it is expected that 2024 will surpass this record, highlighting the acceleration of global warming. Data from the European Copernicus Observatory indicates that on July 22, 2024, the daily global average temperature reached a new record, underscoring the severity of the current climate situation. The effects of global warming are visible and are expected to worsen in the coming decades, including ice loss, rising sea levels, and more intense heat waves.
